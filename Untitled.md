# Comprehensive RIBO Level 1 Exam Study Guide: Mastering Ontario Automobile Insurance

## Introduction to the Guide

This exhaustive research report serves as a specialized, expert-level study companion for the Registered Insurance Brokers of Ontario (RIBO) Level 1 Examination, specifically targeting **Chapter 9: Automobile Insurance**. This document is designed not merely to summarize the source material but to deconstruct the complex regulatory, contractual, and practical frameworks that govern automobile insurance in Ontario.

Automobile insurance represents the single most significant volume of business for the majority of insurance brokers in the province. Consequently, the regulatory body requires a nuanced, almost granular understanding of how the product functions. Ontario’s system is a hybrid of private market delivery and rigorous government oversight, characterized by a specific mix of tort liability and no-fault accident benefits. Mastery of this chapter is non-negotiable for professional competence.

The report is structured into **Three Phases**, mirroring the logical progression from foundational legal principles to complex coverage application and, finally, to claims administration and high-risk market mechanisms.

# Phase 1: The Regulatory and Contractual Foundations of Ontario Auto

## 1.1 The Landscape of Automobile Insurance in Canada

To act as a competent advisor in Ontario, one must first contextualize the Ontario model within the broader Canadian insurance landscape. The delivery of automobile insurance is not uniform across the federation; it is a province-specific jurisdiction resulting in diverse operational models.

### 1.1.1 Private vs. Public Delivery Models

The Canadian market is fundamentally bifurcated into private and government-run systems.

Private Insurance Jurisdictions:

In Ontario, Alberta, and the Atlantic provinces, automobile insurance is transacted through private insurance companies.1 These are profit-seeking entities that compete for market share based on price, service, and product differentiation, albeit within a strictly regulated framework.

- **The Contract:** In most private jurisdictions (like Alberta), the standard contract is the **Standard Policy Form (SPF) No. 1**. However, Ontario is unique. It utilizes a province-specific form known as the **Ontario Automobile Policy: Owner’s Policy (OAP 1)**.1 This distinction is critical for the exam; while the concepts are similar, the specific policy wording of the OAP 1 is what is tested.
    

Compulsory Government Insurance Plans:

In contrast, British Columbia, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba operate under compulsory government insurance plans.1 In these provinces, the provincial government effectively acts as the insurer for mandatory coverages.

- **British Columbia:** The government plan provides included accident benefits and $200,000 third-party liability. Physical damage coverage is optional and can be purchased from the government or private insurers.1
    
- **Saskatchewan:** Provides included accident benefits and $200,000 liability, with physical damage subject to a $500 deductible.1
    
- **Manitoba:** Similar structure with a $400 deductible for physical damage.1
    

Because these provinces have a monopoly on the basic mandatory coverage, they do not utilize the **Facility Association** (the high-risk pool used in private markets). Brokers in Ontario must understand this distinction, particularly when advising clients moving to or from these provinces.

The Quebec Hybrid Model:

Quebec operates a unique dual system. The government maintains a monopoly on personal injury coverage (Bodily Injury) for all accidents occurring in the province. However, property damage liability and physical damage coverage for the vehicle itself must be purchased from private insurers.1

### 1.1.2 The "Take All Comers" Rule

In Ontario, automobile insurance is mandatory. Because the government compels citizens to purchase the product, it must ensure the product is available to them. This necessity gave rise to the **Take All Comers (TAC)** rule.

The TAC rule prohibits insurers from declining to issue, forcing the cancellation of, or refusing to renew a basic automobile insurance policy for any eligible applicant who meets the underwriting rules the insurer has filed with the regulator (FSRA).1

Implications for Brokers:

The burden of the TAC rule falls heavily on the broker. A broker is obligated to survey all available markets with which they have a contract.

- **Scenario:** A client, Jarrod, has three not-at-fault claims. His current insurer refuses to renew him, citing "too many claims," but the insurer has _not_ filed a rule with FSRA explicitly stating that three not-at-fault claims are grounds for declination.
    
- **Outcome:** The insurer is in violation of the TAC rule. The broker must document this refusal and, ideally, force the insurer to comply or report the conduct.1
    
- **Documentation:** If a broker cannot find a quote from their markets, they must meticulously document which insurers were approached and the specific reasons for declination. This protects the broker against Errors and Omissions (E&O) claims and regulatory scrutiny.1
    

### 1.1.3 No-Fault Concepts: Pure vs. Threshold

The term "No-Fault" is a frequent source of confusion. It refers to the _compensation model_, not the assignment of blame for the accident.

- **Pure No-Fault (Manitoba/Quebec):** In these jurisdictions, the right to sue for injuries is effectively removed. Victims are compensated solely according to a government schedule of benefits, regardless of who caused the accident.
    
- **Threshold No-Fault (Ontario):** Ontario uses a hybrid "threshold" system.
    
    - **Accident Benefits:** All injured persons claim medical and income benefits from their _own_ insurer, regardless of fault.1
        
    - **Right to Sue (Tort):** An innocent victim retains the right to sue the at-fault driver, but only if their injuries meet a specific severity "threshold" (e.g., permanent serious disfigurement or serious impairment of an important physical, mental, or psychological function).1
        

## 1.2 The Application for Automobile Insurance (OAF 1)

The insurance contract begins well before the policy is issued; it begins with the application. In Ontario, the standard form is the **Ontario Application for Automobile Insurance: Owner’s Form (OAF 1)**. This document is the foundation of the underwriting process. The Insurance Act stipulates that the application forms part of the contract itself.1

### 1.2.1 Detailed Analysis of Application Sections

Section 1: Name and Postal Address

The applicant must provide their full legal name and address. This is not merely for billing; it establishes the territory for rating purposes.

- **Risk Assessment:** Insurers assume the vehicle is chiefly operated in the vicinity of the residence. If an applicant lives in a low-risk rural area but uses the vehicle to commute daily to downtown Toronto, this "commute" information must be disclosed to ensure proper rating.1
    
- **Driver History:** The name and address are used to pull the "Driver Abstract" (MVR) from the Ministry of Transportation. Inaccuracies here can lead to incorrect premium calculations or failure to uncover poor driving records.1
    

Section 2: Policy Period

Coverage binds at the time of application and expires at 12:01 a.m. local time at the insured's address.

- **Time Zone Criticality:** The definition of "local time" is vital in a province like Ontario, which spans time zones (though mostly Eastern, areas near the Manitoba border are Central). If a policy expires at 12:01 a.m., a crash at 12:05 a.m. is uninsured. This precision prevents coverage gaps during renewals or insurer switches.1
    

Section 3: Details of the Described Automobile

This section captures the physical identity of the risk (VIN, Make, Model).

- **Lienholders:** The applicant must disclose any lienholders or lessors. This protects the financial interest of the lender. If a vehicle is a total loss, the check is made co-payable to the insured and the lienholder.
    
- **Usage Variables:** The application asks for:
    
    - One-way commuting distance.
        
    - Annual total mileage.
        
    - Business use.
        
    - Carpooling details.
        
    - _Insight:_ A change in these variables (e.g., getting a new job 40km further away) constitutes a "Material Change in Risk" and must be reported.1
        
- **Modifications:** The OAF 1 specifically asks about modifications. There is a special note limiting coverage for after-market electronics to **$1,500** unless otherwise endorsed.1
    

Section 4: Driver Information

Every person who regularly or occasionally operates the vehicle must be listed.

- **Retiree Discount:** The OAF 1 includes specific questions to qualify for the "Retiree Discount." The principal operator must be retired, age 65+ (or receiving a pension), have no earned income, and not be employed for 26 of the last 52 weeks. This is a mandatory discount if the criteria are met.1
    
- **Excluded Drivers:** This section allows the underwriter to identify high-risk household members (e.g., a teen with a DUI). The insurer may demand they be excluded from the policy (using OPCF 28A) to avoid a premium surcharge or declination.1
    

Section 5 & 6: Accident and Conviction History

The applicant must disclose:

- **Accidents (6 Years):** All incidents, regardless of fault.
    
- **Convictions (3 Years):** All Highway Traffic Act convictions (speeding, careless driving).
    
- _Insight:_ The disparity in timeframes (6 years for claims vs. 3 years for convictions) is a common exam trick. Brokers must ensure clients go back the full six years for claims history.1
    

Section 11: Declaration of Applicant

The applicant signs to warrant the truth of the statements.

- **The Warning:** The application contains a bold warning regarding false statements. Misrepresentation of facts (e.g., omitting a teenage driver, lying about address) forfeits the right to recover under the policy. This forfeiture applies to the applicant's own claims (e.g., theft of their car) but usually leaves the "Absolute Liability" protection for innocent third parties intact.1
    

## 1.3 The Policy Contract (OAP 1): General Provisions

The **OAP 1 (Owner's Policy)** is the contract that binds the insurer and the insured. It is written in "plain language" to be accessible to the consumer, yet it carries the weight of statute.

### 1.3.1 Contract Components

Section 1.1 states that the contract consists of three distinct documents:

1. The **Application** (OAF 1).
    
2. The **Certificate of Automobile Insurance** (The "Dec Page" listing limits and premiums).
    
3. The **Policy Wording** (OAP 1) itself.1
    

### 1.3.2 Where You Are Covered (Territory)

The OAP 1 provides coverage in **Canada**, the **United States**, and on a vessel traveling between ports of those countries.1

- _Exclusion:_ Coverage does _not_ extend to Mexico or other countries. Clients driving to Mexico must purchase separate Mexican insurance.
    

### 1.3.3 Section 1.4: Your Responsibilities

The policy explicitly lists actions the insured must avoid. Violation of these responsibilities is a breach of contract.

- **Notification:** The insured must notify the insurer promptly of any significant change in the status of the driver, owner, or lessee. This links directly to **Statutory Condition 1 (Material Change in Risk)**.1
    
- **Unauthorized Driving:** The insured agrees not to drive unless authorized by law (i.e., must have a valid license). Driving while suspended breaches the policy.1
    
- **Prohibited Use:** The insured agrees not to use the vehicle for:
    
    - Racing or speed tests.
        
    - Illegal trade or transportation (e.g., smuggling).
        
    - Carrying paying passengers (unless endorsed).1
        
    - _Insight:_ "Carpooling" is explicitly _allowed_ and is not considered carrying paying passengers, provided the payment is only to share costs and not for profit.1
        

## 1.4 Mandatory Coverages Analysis

The **Automobile Insurance Rate Stability Act (1996)** mandates that every motor vehicle liability policy in Ontario must include four specific coverages: Liability, Accident Benefits, Uninsured Automobile, and Direct Compensation–Property Damage.

### 1.4.1 Section 3: Liability Coverage

This section indemnifies the insured when they are sued by a third party for bodily injury or property damage caused by the insured vehicle.

**Limits and Priority:**

- **Minimum Limit:** The statutory minimum in Ontario is **$200,000**.1
    
- **Priority of Claims:** If a judgment exceeds the policy limit (e.g., a $300,000 judgment on a $200,000 policy), the law mandates a priority split: **95% ($190,000)** is reserved for Bodily Injury, and **5% ($10,000)** is reserved for Property Damage.1
    
- _Insight:_ This ensures that victims with physical injuries are prioritized over property damage claims when funds are scarce.
    

Defense Costs:

Crucially, the insurer agrees to pay the costs of defending the lawsuit (legal fees, court costs) in addition to the limit of liability. Post-judgment interest is also covered, but only on the portion of the judgment that falls within the policy limit.1

Rented and Leased Vehicles (Section 3.3.5):

Since 2007, legislation has reordered the priority of insurance for rental cars.

- **Scenario:** Amelia rents a car from Hertz. She causes an accident.
    
- **Priority:**
    
    1. **First Payer:** Amelia’s own OAP 1 (under her "Liability for Non-Owned Automobiles" provision).
        
    2. **Second Payer:** Any policy where Amelia is a listed driver.
        
    3. **Last Payer:** The Rental Company’s (Hertz) insurance policy.
        
- _Implication:_ The rental company’s insurance is now purely "excess" or contingent. Brokers must ensure clients have adequate transferable coverage on their personal policies.1
    

### 1.4.2 Section 4: Statutory Accident Benefits (SABS)

This is the "No-Fault" component. It provides benefits to the insured, passengers, and pedestrians struck by the vehicle. The benefits are defined by regulation (Regulation 403/96) and are categorized by injury severity.

**The Three Injury Categories:**

1. **Minor Injury:** Sprains, strains, whiplash.
    
    - **Medical/Rehab Limit:** Capped at **$3,500**.1
        
2. **Non-Catastrophic Injury:** Severe fractures, internal injuries (that do not meet catastrophic criteria).
    
    - **Medical/Rehab/Attendant Care Limit:** Combined limit of **$65,000** available for up to 5 years.1
        
3. **Catastrophic Impairment:** Paraplegia, amputation, blindness, severe brain injury.
    
    - **Medical/Rehab/Attendant Care Limit:** Combined limit of **$1,000,000** available for life.1
        

**Key Benefits Breakdown:**

- **Income Replacement Benefit:**
    
    - Pays **70% of gross income** up to a maximum of **$400/week**.
        
    - Wait period: No payment for the first **7 days**.
        
    - Duration: Up to 104 weeks (2 years). Can extend beyond 104 weeks only if the person suffers a "complete inability" to engage in _any_ employment.1
        
- **Non-Earner Benefit:**
    
    - For students or unemployed persons who cannot carry on a normal life.
        
    - Pays **$185/week** after a 4-week waiting period.1
        
- **Caregiver Benefit:**
    
    - Pays for a caregiver if the insured was the primary caregiver for a dependant.
        
    - _Restriction:_ Only available for **Catastrophic Injuries** in the standard policy (can be purchased optionally for all injuries).1
        
- **Death and Funeral Benefit:**
    
    - Death Benefit: **$25,000** to a spouse, **$10,000** to each dependant.
        
    - Funeral Benefit: Maximum **$6,000**.1
        

Optional Benefits (The Upsell):

Because standard SABS limits are often insufficient for high-income earners or those seeking broader protection, brokers must offer optional increases:

- Income Replacement increased to $600, $800, or $1,000/week.
    
- Medical/Rehab for Catastrophic injuries increased to $2 Million.
    
- Caregiver benefit extended to Non-Catastrophic injuries.
    
- Indexation benefit (adjusting benefits for inflation).1
    

### 1.4.3 Section 5: Uninsured Automobile Coverage

This coverage is a safety net for when the mandatory insurance system fails—specifically, when the at-fault driver has no insurance or cannot be identified.

**Two Distinct Triggers:**

1. **Bodily Injury:**
    
    - Covers the insured if injured by an **uninsured** driver OR an **unidentified** (hit-and-run) driver.
        
    - Limit: The provincial minimum liability limit (**$200,000** in Ontario).1
        
2. **Property Damage:**
    
    - Covers damage to the insured vehicle caused by an **uninsured** driver.
        
    - _Critical Requirement:_ The at-fault driver must be **identified**. Coverage does _not_ apply to hit-and-run property damage (to prevent fraud). Hit-and-run damage to the vehicle must be claimed under optional Collision coverage.
        
    - Limit: Maximum **$25,000**, subject to a **$300 deductible**.1
        

### 1.4.4 Section 6: Direct Compensation–Property Damage (DCPD)

DCPD is the mechanism that allows Ontario’s "no-fault" property damage system to function. It streamlines claims by ensuring an insured deals with their _own_ insurer for vehicle damage, even if they are not at fault.

Qualifying Criteria:

For a claim to be settled under DCPD, strictly three conditions must be met:

1. The accident occurred in **Ontario**.
    
2. Both vehicles are insured by licensed insurers (or signatories to the DCPD agreement).
    
3. The accident involved at least one other vehicle (cannot be single-vehicle).1
    

The Mechanics of Compensation:

The insurer pays for vehicle damage and Loss of Use (rental car) based on the degree to which the insured is not at fault.

- **100% Not at Fault:** Insurer pays 100% of repairs. Standard deductible is $0 (though $500 is common if selected).
    
- **50% At Fault:** Insurer pays 50% of the repairs under DCPD. The remaining 50% must be claimed under Section 7 (Collision), subject to the Collision deductible.
    
- _Insight:_ This allows good drivers to avoid paying deductibles when they are innocent victims, while ensuring their own insurer handles the repair process efficiently.1

# Phase 2: Asset Protection and Policy Customization

While Phase 1 focused on the mandatory minimums required to drive legally, Phase 2 explores how the policy is expanded to protect the asset itself and tailored to specific client needs through endorsements.

## 2.1 Section 7: Loss or Damage Coverages (Optional)

This section, commonly referred to as "Physical Damage," indemnifies the insured for loss of or damage to the described automobile. Unlike DCPD, this coverage applies when the insured is at fault, or when the accident does not involve another insured vehicle (e.g., hitting a tree).

### 2.1.1 The Four Coverage Subsets

The policy offers four levels of protection, each progressively broader.

|**Coverage Option**|**Description and Scope**|**Key Exclusions/Notes**|
|---|---|---|
|**A. Specified Perils**|The most restrictive "Named Perils" form. Covers Fire, Theft, Lightning, Windstorm, Hail, Earthquake, Explosion, Riot, Forced Landing of Aircraft, Stranding/Sinking of Transport.|Does _not_ cover vandalism, falling objects, or collision. Rarely sold alone except for antique/stored vehicles.|
|**B. Comprehensive**|An "All-Risk" coverage excluding Collision. Covers all perils in 'A' plus Vandalism, Falling Objects, Missiles.|Explicitly **excludes** theft by a person residing in the same household or an employee.|
|**C. Collision or Upset**|Covers damage caused by collision with another object (vehicle, tree, road surface) or by "upset" (tipping over).|This is the primary coverage for at-fault accidents and single-vehicle crashes.|
|**D. All Perils**|The broadest possible coverage. Combines Collision and Comprehensive.|**Crucial Distinction:** Unlike Comprehensive, All Perils **includes** theft by a person in the household or an employee.|

### 2.1.2 Deductibles and Their Application

Most Section 7 coverages are subject to a deductible—the amount the insured must contribute to the loss.

- **Standard:** Typically $500 or $1,000.
    
- **Fire & Lightning Exception:** The policy dictates that no deductible applies to losses caused by **Fire** or **Lightning**. This is a standard consumer protection feature.1
    
- **The 50/50 Crash Scenario:**
    
    - _Situation:_ Jamal is 50% at fault in an accident causing $4,000 damage.
        
    - _Calculation:_
        
        1. **DCPD (Section 6):** Pays the 50% not-at-fault portion ($2,000). Deductible: 50% of the DCPD deductible applies ($250). Net DCPD Payment: $1,750.
            
        2. **Collision (Section 7):** Pays the 50% at-fault portion ($2,000). Deductible: 50% of the Collision deductible applies ($250). Net Collision Payment: $1,750.
            
    - _Total Payout:_ $3,500. Total Deductible Paid by Jamal: $500.1
        

### 2.1.3 Additional Benefits within Section 7

Beyond fixing the metal, Section 7 includes supplementary benefits to reduce the insured's burden.

1. Loss of Use Due to Theft:

If the vehicle is stolen, and the insured has Theft coverage (Specified Perils, Comprehensive, or All Perils), the insurer pays for a rental car or alternate transportation.

- **Limit:** Maximum **$900** total.
    
- **Waiting Period:** Coverage begins **72 hours** after the theft is reported to the police/insurer.
    
- **Duration:** Continues until the vehicle is repaired, replaced, or the claim is settled.1
    

2. General Average and Salvage Charges:

The policy covers liability for "General Average" charges. This is a marine law concept. If a ferry carrying the insured vehicle is in danger of sinking and jettisons some cars to save the ship, the owners of the saved cars must contribute to the loss of the jettisoned ones. The OAP 1 pays this contribution.1

3. Temporary Substitute Automobile (TSA):

If the insured drives a "loaner" vehicle while their described auto is broken down or being repaired, Section 7 coverage extends to that loaner.

- **Mechanic:** If the loaner has no insurance, the OAP 1 pays. If the loaner _has_ insurance but with a higher deductible (e.g., Garage has a $2,500 deductible, Insured has $500), the OAP 1 pays the difference ($2,000).1
    

## 2.2 Ontario Policy Change Forms (OPCF) - The Endorsements

The standard OAP 1 is a rigid chassis; OPCFs are the modifications that tailor it to the specific driver. The source text lists numerous forms; the following analysis focuses on the high-probability exam topics and their strategic utility.

### 2.2.1 Critical Coverage Extensions

**OPCF 2: Drive Other Automobiles**

- **Problem:** The OAP 1 only covers the Named Insured and Spouse for driving non-owned vehicles (Section 2.2.3). It does _not_ cover listed drivers (e.g., children).
    
- **Solution:** OPCF 2 extends this coverage to specific named persons, ensuring they are protected when driving a friend's car.1
    

**OPCF 20: Coverage for Transportation Replacement**

- **Problem:** The standard policy only covers rental cars for _Theft_ (and DCPD claims). If an insured is at-fault in a crash and their car is in the shop for weeks, they have no rental coverage.
    
- **Solution:** OPCF 20 pays for a rental vehicle for _any_ insured loss (collision, fire, etc.). It is a critical upsell for daily commuters.1
    

**OPCF 27: Liability for Damage to Non-Owned Automobile(s)**

- **Problem:** Rental car agencies charge exorbitant fees for "Collision Damage Waivers."
    
- **Solution:** OPCF 27 extends the insured’s physical damage coverage to a rented vehicle (in Canada/USA). It effectively transfers the insured's own policy coverage to the rental, usually with a limit (e.g., $50,000).1
    

**OPCF 43: Removing Depreciation Deduction**

- **Problem:** A new car loses value the moment it is driven off the lot. A total loss in month 6 would result in a payout thousands less than the loan amount (ACV vs. MSRP).
    
- **Solution:** OPCF 43 waives the depreciation deduction for the first 24-30 months. In a total loss, the insurer pays the original purchase price or the MSRP of a new replacement, whichever is _lower_.1
    

**OPCF 44R: Family Protection Endorsement**

- **Why Vital:** This is arguably the most important endorsement for personal protection.
    
- **Mechanism:** It covers the gap if the insured or their family is hit by an at-fault driver who has insufficient liability limits.
    
    - _Scenario:_ Insured has $1 Million liability coverage. They are hit by a driver with only $200,000 coverage. The insured suffers $800,000 in injuries.
        
    - _Outcome:_ The at-fault driver's policy pays the first $200,000. OPCF 44R on the insured's own policy pays the remaining $600,000 (up to the insured's own limit).1
        

### 2.2.2 Risk Management and Restriction Endorsements

**OPCF 16: Suspension of Coverage**

- **Use Case:** A client stores their convertible for the winter.
    
- **Function:** Suspends road coverages (Liability, Collision) and refunds a portion of the premium. Comprehensive (fire/theft) remains active.
    
- **Reinstatement:** Requires **OPCF 17** to reactivate coverage in the spring.1
    

**OPCF 28A: Excluded Driver**

- **Use Case:** A household member (e.g., son with multiple DUIs) makes the policy uninsurable or prohibitively expensive.
    
- **Function:** The Named Insured signs to explicitly exclude this driver. If the excluded driver operates the vehicle, there is **NO coverage** for liability or property damage. Only limited SABS benefits apply to the excluded driver.
    
- **Warning:** Both the insured and the excluded driver must sign this form.1
    

**OPCF 49: Agreement Not to Recover for Loss or Damage (DCPD Opt-Out)**

- **Context:** Introduced in January 2024 to give consumers a cost-saving option.
    
- **Function:** The insured agrees to receive **$0 compensation** for damage to their vehicle, even if they are 100% not at fault in an accident.
    
- **Risk:** This removes the DCPD protection entirely. It is suitable only for vehicles with negligible value ("beaters") where the owner is prepared to walk away from a total loss.1

# Phase 3: Claims, Conditions, and System Administration

The final phase examines the operational rules of the contract—what happens after the policy is sold, specifically regarding claims obligations, cancellations, and mechanisms for high-risk drivers.

## 3.1 Statutory Conditions

The **Insurance Act** mandates 13 Statutory Conditions be printed in every automobile policy. These are the "Rules of the Road" for the contract. A breach of these conditions can lead to the denial of a claim and termination of the policy.

### 3.1.1 Critical Conditions for Brokers

Condition 1: Material Change in Risk

The insured is legally obligated to notify the insurer promptly of any change that increases the risk.

- _Examples:_ Changing the vehicle's use from pleasure to Uber/Lyft; a new licensed driver moving into the home; moving to a different city.
    
- _Consequence:_ Failure to report can void the policy ab initio (from the beginning) regarding a claim.1
    

Condition 4: Authority to Drive

The insured shall not drive unless authorized by law (must have a valid license). Nor shall they permit anyone else to drive unless that person is authorized.

- _Prohibitions:_ The vehicle cannot be used for illicit trade (smuggling) or racing.
    

Condition 5: Requirements Where Loss or Damage to Persons or Property

This condition governs Liability claims.

- **Notice:** The insured must notify the insurer _in writing_ within **7 days** of the incident.
    
- **No Admission:** The insured must _not_ voluntarily assume liability or settle any claim (e.g., offering cash to the other driver at the roadside). Doing so prejudices the insurer’s defense and violates the contract.1
    

Condition 6: Requirements Where Loss or Damage to the Automobile

This condition governs Physical Damage claims.

- **Protection:** The insured must protect the automobile from further damage (e.g., towing it to a safe yard, not leaving it on the highway). The insurer pays for these reasonable costs.
    
- **Proof of Loss:** The insured must submit a Statutory Declaration (Proof of Loss) within **90 days** of the accident.
    
- **No Abandonment:** The insured cannot simply abandon the wreck to the insurer. The insurer has the option to repair, replace, or pay Actual Cash Value (ACV).1
    

Condition 11: Termination

This defines how the contract can be ended.

- **By Insured:** Can cancel at any time. Refund is calculated on a **Short Rate** basis (insurer retains a penalty/handling cost).
    
- **By Insurer:**
    
    - **Reason:** Must be valid (e.g., Non-Payment, Misrepresentation).
        
    - **Non-Payment of Premium:** Requires **10 days** notice (hand-delivered) or **30 days** notice (registered mail).
        
    - **Other Reasons:** Requires **5 days** notice (hand-delivered) or **15 days** notice (registered mail).
        
    - **Refund:** Calculated on a **Pro-Rata** basis (full refund for unused days).1
        

## 3.2 The Facility Association ("The Pool")

In a mandatory insurance market, a mechanism must exist for drivers who do not meet the underwriting standards of the voluntary private market (e.g., multiple at-fault accidents or criminal convictions).

Structure and Function:

The Facility Association (FA) is not an insurance company; it is an unincorporated association of all automobile insurers in the province. It acts as a "pool" to share the losses of high-risk drivers.

- **Servicing Carriers:** The FA does not issue policies directly. It designates specific "Servicing Carriers" (regular insurers) to issue policies and handle claims on its behalf.
    
- **The Broker's Duty:** Brokers must always attempt to place a risk in the regular (voluntary) market first. Placing a client in the FA is a last resort. The broker must usually provide rejection letters from voluntary markets to qualify the client for FA.1
    
- **Shared Results:** The financial results (usually losses) of the FA are pooled and shared among all insurers in proportion to their market share in the province.1
    

## 3.3 Specialized Policy Forms: OAP 2 and OAP 4

While the OAP 1 covers owned vehicles, other forms cover specific exposures.

OAP 2: Driver's Policy

This policy is for individuals who drive but do not own a vehicle.

- _Target Audience:_ Government employees driving fleet cars, drivers of company cars who need personal protection, or high-risk drivers excluded from a spouse's policy who need proof of insurance to maintain a license.
    
- _Coverage:_ Provides Liability and Accident Benefits for non-owned vehicles.1
    

OAP 4: Garage Policy

Designed for businesses that take custody of customers' vehicles (Garages, Body Shops, Valet Services).

- _The Bailment Gap:_ A standard OAP 1 explicitly excludes damage to property in the care, custody, or control of the insured. Therefore, a mechanic who crashes a customer's car during a test drive is _not_ covered by their personal auto policy.
    
- _The Fix:_ The OAP 4 covers "Liability for Damage to Customer’s Automobile," closing this critical gap for businesses.1
    

## 3.4 Dispute Resolution

The **Financial Services Regulatory Authority of Ontario (FSRA)** provides a framework for resolving disputes, particularly regarding Accident Benefits (SABS), to prevent the court system from being overwhelmed.

- **Process:** Disputes first go to mediation. If unresolved, they may proceed to arbitration or litigation.
    
- **MVACF:** The **Motor Vehicle Accident Claims Fund** acts as the "payer of last resort." If a pedestrian is hit by a hit-and-run driver and has no insurance of their own, the MVACF provides benefits (up to $200,000) and then attempts to recover costs from the at-fault party if identified.1
